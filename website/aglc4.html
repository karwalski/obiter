What is AGLC4?

A plain-language introduction to the citation standard used across Australian legal writing.

The Australian Guide to Legal Citation

The Australian Guide to Legal Citation (AGLC) is the standard citation system used across Australian law schools, courts, and legal publications. It provides a uniform set of rules for referencing legal materials so that any reader can locate the exact source being cited.

Now in its 4th edition — commonly referred to as AGLC4 — the guide is published by the Melbourne University Law Review and the Melbourne Journal of International Law. If you are familiar with citation systems in other countries, AGLC4 is the Australian equivalent of the Bluebook in the United States or OSCOLA in the United Kingdom.

In practical terms, AGLC4 tells legal writers exactly how to format references to cases, legislation, books, journal articles, treaties, and dozens of other source types. It specifies everything from the order of elements in a citation to the use of italics, punctuation, and abbreviations.

Why Does Citation Matter?

Legal arguments depend on authority. Every claim in a legal document — whether it is a court judgment, an academic article, or a student essay — must be backed by a source. Citation is the mechanism that connects an argument to its supporting authority.

  • Verification. Consistent citation allows readers to quickly find and verify the sources that support an argument. A properly formatted citation acts as a precise address for a legal source.
  • Credibility. Incorrect or inconsistent citations can undermine the credibility of legal writing. Courts and journals expect citations to follow the accepted standard.
  • Clarity. A uniform citation system eliminates ambiguity. When every writer follows the same rules, readers can focus on the substance of an argument rather than decoding its references.
  • Universal requirement. Law students, academics, and practitioners all need to cite correctly. AGLC4 is the standard taught in virtually every Australian law school and required by most Australian legal journals.

What Does AGLC4 Cover?

AGLC4 is organised into five parts spanning 26 chapters. Together, they cover approximately 80 distinct source types and hundreds of specific formatting rules.

Part I: General Rules

The foundational rules that apply to all citations: how footnotes work, punctuation conventions, the use of italics and emphasis, pinpoint references, short titles, subsequent references (including the ibid rule), and bibliography formatting.

Part II: Domestic Sources

Rules for citing Australian cases and legislation. This includes reported and unreported cases, legislation from all Australian jurisdictions, bills, explanatory memoranda, and parliamentary materials such as Hansard.

Part III: Secondary Sources

Rules for non-primary legal materials: books, edited collections, journal articles, reports, government documents, newspaper articles, internet sources, speeches, and other written materials.

Part IV: International Materials

Rules for citing international treaties, United Nations documents, International Court of Justice decisions, World Trade Organization materials, European Union instruments, and other international sources.

Part V: Foreign Domestic Sources

Rules for citing domestic materials from foreign jurisdictions, including the United Kingdom, the United States, New Zealand, Canada, and nine other countries. Each jurisdiction has its own specific conventions.

The Problem Obiter Solves

AGLC4 has hundreds of rules, and no one memorises them all. Even experienced legal writers regularly need to consult the guide when citing unfamiliar source types or navigating the more detailed formatting requirements.

Existing reference management tools such as Zotero and EndNote provide partial support, but they cover only about 30% of the source types in AGLC4. The remaining 70% must be formatted manually — a process that is tedious, error-prone, and time-consuming.

Obiter is a free, open-source Microsoft Word add-in that automates the entire AGLC4 standard. It handles everything from basic case citations to complex treaty references, manages ibid logic and cross-references automatically, generates correctly formatted bibliographies, and validates your document against the full set of AGLC4 rules — all without leaving Microsoft Word.

Beyond AGLC4

Obiter is expanding to support additional citation standards, including OSCOLA (Oxford University Standard for the Citation of Legal Authorities, 5th edition) and the New Zealand Law Style Guide (NZLSG). The same engine and workflow will allow users to switch between Australian, UK, and NZ citation standards on a per-document basis.

AGLC4 remains the primary and most comprehensive implementation. It is the standard against which the engine was designed and tested, and it will continue to receive the deepest coverage and the earliest updates.
