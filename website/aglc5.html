Supporting AGLC5 Adoption

An open letter requesting collaboration between the AGLC5 Committee and the Obiter project to ensure citation tooling is ready from day one.

To:

  • The Melbourne University Law Review Association
  • The Melbourne Journal of International Law
  • The AGLC5 Committee

Since its first edition in 1998, the Australian Guide to Legal Citation has served as the authoritative standard for legal citation in Australia. Across four editions, the AGLC has provided the consistency and rigour that legal scholarship demands. The forthcoming fifth edition, currently under development, represents an opportunity not only to refine the standard but to consider how that standard is implemented in the tools that legal professionals use every day.

Obiter: Open-Source AGLC4 Implementation

Obiter is a free, open-source Microsoft Word add-in that implements the full AGLC4 specification across all 26 chapters. It automates footnote formatting, ibid and cross-reference resolution, pinpoint references, bibliography generation, and document-wide validation. It is licensed under the GNU General Public License v3.0 and is developed in the open for the benefit of the entire legal community.

The process of building Obiter has provided a detailed understanding of AGLC4 at the level of individual rules, exceptions, and edge cases. That experience has made clear that citation standards and citation tools are most effective when they are developed in concert rather than in isolation.

The Case for Concurrent Development

Citation guides have historically been published as static documents, with software implementations following months or years later. This approach creates an adoption gap: users are expected to apply a new standard manually while waiting for their tools to catch up. It also means that rules which are difficult or ambiguous to automate are discovered only after publication, when changes are costly and disruptive.

Modern citation tooling should be designed alongside the standard it implements, not retrofitted after the fact. Developing software in parallel with the rules it encodes surfaces practical implementation issues early, while they can still be addressed through minor clarifications or adjustments to drafting.

Request for Early Preview Access

We respectfully request early preview access to draft AGLC5 rules for the purpose of software development and implementation testing. This access would enable the Obiter project to:

  • Support AGLC5 from day one of publication. Obiter's architecture is already version-parameterised, with a dedicated rules directory prepared for AGLC5. Early access would allow a working implementation to be available to users on the date of publication, eliminating the usual lag between a new edition and tool support.
  • Provide practical implementation feedback. Translating citation rules into deterministic software logic is a rigorous test of their clarity and completeness. Where a rule proves difficult to automate within Microsoft Word — whether due to ambiguity, implicit assumptions, or typographic constraints — we would provide structured feedback to the Committee, identifying the specific issue and, where possible, suggesting alternative formulations that preserve the rule's intent while improving implementability.
  • Enable seamless transition for users. Many AGLC users will need to migrate existing documents from AGLC4 to AGLC5 formatting. Early access would allow Obiter to develop migration tooling and validation checks that identify AGLC4-specific formatting and guide users through the transition, reducing the burden on individual researchers, students, and practitioners.

Invitation to Collaborate

The Obiter project is open source and welcomes contributions from the Committee, the Melbourne University Law Review, and the broader legal community. We invite collaboration in whatever form is most appropriate: from formal partnership to informal feedback on draft implementations.

We are prepared to offer technical resources and citation engine expertise to support the AGLC5 development process. This includes detailed analysis of how specific rules translate into software logic, identification of edge cases that may benefit from additional guidance, and testing of draft rules against real-world citation patterns drawn from Australian legal scholarship.

The AGLC has been an indispensable resource for Australian legal citation for over 25 years. We believe that AGLC5, developed with the benefit of concurrent software implementation, can set a new standard not only for citation accuracy but for citation accessibility.

Respectfully,

Matthew Watt
Author of Obiter
