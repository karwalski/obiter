Supporting AGLC5 Adoption

An open letter requesting collaboration between the AGLC5 Committee and the Obiter project to ensure citation tooling is ready from day one.

To:

  • The Melbourne University Law Review Association
  • The Melbourne Journal of International Law
  • The AGLC5 Committee

Two independent AGLC4 citation-style patches — both generated with the assistance of Anthropic's Claude — are currently hosted on GitHub and being used by Australian legal researchers. One practitioner, working on an 80,000-word research piece, described the situation candidly on the Zotero forums: “I used Claude to modify the CSL for AGLC4 to fix the issues I had noticed. After several iterations it is working and I have loaded it into my Zotero installation. It still isn't fully compliant with the AGLC4 guide, but pretty good.” The patches exist because the official AGLC4 Citation Style Language file has not been updated since 2020, and because the dual medium-neutral-citation-plus-authorised-report format that every Federal Court practitioner is expected to produce cannot be emitted by that file at all.

This letter is not a criticism of the AGLC. Since its first edition in 1998, the Australian Guide to Legal Citation has provided the consistency and rigour that Australian legal scholarship depends on, and its influence across courts, universities, and journals is unmatched. But the experience of building Obiter — a free, open-source Microsoft Word add-in implementing the full AGLC4 specification across all 26 chapters — has made clear that AGLC4 was drafted without any model of its software implementation, and that the cost of that gap is now measured in years of patches, workarounds, and institutional divergence. The forthcoming fifth edition represents a closing window in which the next AGLC can be the first one that treats implementability as a drafting constraint rather than a post-publication salvage operation.

Specific Rule Failures

The following AGLC4 rules have documented, unresolved implementation failures across every major citation tool:

  • Rules 1.4.1 and 1.4.4 — Subsequent references and short titles. The first bug filed against the AGLC4 CSL style on its release day in April 2019 concerned subsequent case references. Seven years later, the same rule family remains broken: short titles render in italics when the parent title is roman (violating Rule 1.4.4), and Rule 1.4.1 makes short titles optional (“may be used”) but offers no computable criterion for when they should be. The AGLC4 preface (page xii) acknowledges the (n X) cross-reference system is “facultative rather than mandatory” but provides no toggle a tool author can expose to users.
  • Rule 2.1.14 — Subsequent case references. Users reported outputs such as “Victoria Park Racing and Recreation Grounds Company Ltd v Taylor (n 1 495, 497–498)” when AGLC requires “Victoria Park Racing (n 1) 495, 497–498.” The fix took four months to ship. Related defects — paragraph pinpoints ignoring the square-bracket convention, Bill item types wrapping subsequent references in stray single quotes — remain open.
  • Rule 2.2.1 and 2.2.7 — Reported cases and parallel citations. AGLC4 forbids parallel citation. Every practitioner-facing court in Australia requires it. The Supreme Court of Queensland Practice Direction 1 of 2024 directs that where a judgment appears in an authorised report series, both the medium neutral citation and the authorised citation should be noted. Populating both fields in Zotero causes the citator to produce mangled output. This is the defect that drove two separate users to generate AI-assisted CSL patches.
  • Rule 3.1.7 — Short titles for portions of Acts. This rule creates a conceptual conflation that no metadata schema can resolve: the same field must carry both “short title for the whole Act” and “short title for a portion of the Act” with no way for the processor to distinguish between them.
  • Rule 3.5 — Subsequent legislation references. The style uses the “Name of Act” field as a pseudo-short title even when no short title exists, producing outputs like “Public Health Act (n 81)” — fatal ambiguity when every Australian jurisdiction has a Public Health Act.
  • Rule 7.12 — Written correspondence (and the GenAI workaround). AGLC4 predates ChatGPT and contains no rule for generative AI citation. The Melbourne University Law Review editors issued interim guidance advising that AI outputs be cited broadly following Rule 7.12. Every major Australian university law library — UWA, UQ, Macquarie, Adelaide, SCU, Deakin, La Trobe, ACU and others — has propagated this workaround through their LibGuides, each noting that it is “interim guidance” and not officially part of AGLC4. No AGLC4 tool has ever been able to emit a correct GenAI citation because no rule authorises one.
  • Rules 8–14 and Rule 8.6 — International materials. The entire international chapter — treaties, UN materials, ICJ/PCIJ decisions, international arbitration, international criminal tribunals, and supranational materials — has no CSL implementation. The official maintainer's explanation is frank: “Zotero simply doesn't have the ability to properly store/identify such materials and so we can't implement them in any citation style.” Memoranda of Understanding (Rule 8.6) have no representation at all. Putting type: treaty in Zotero's Extra field actually breaks AGLC4 output — the opposite of how other international styles behave.

These are not marginal edge cases. They are the rules that legal researchers encounter daily, and they have been documented in public forum threads, bug reports, and community patches for seven years.

Structural Limits of Citation Style Language

The problems above are not solvable by incremental bug fixes. Citation Style Language itself has known structural limits that the CSL project openly acknowledges: “Limited support for legal styles. The official version of CSL still has limited support for citing legal materials.” The specific barriers are architectural, not accidental:

  • Content-testing of variables is forbidden by the CSL specification. AGLC4's round-versus-square-bracket rule — round for reports organised by volume, square for reports organised by year, and the Victorian Reports switching from square to round in 2000 — requires a per-reporter lookup that no standard CSL field exists to feed. This is not a bug. It is the architecture.
  • “Below n” cross-references are architecturally impossible. CSL's citation processor is stateful and forward-only: a reference to note 12 cannot be emitted at note 3 because note 12 does not yet exist in the document state when note 3 is processed. AGLC4 Rule 1.4.2 allows both “above n” and “below n” cross-references; no compliant CSL processor can ever produce the “below n” form automatically.
  • Parallel citations require Juris-M extensions that Zotero will not merge. Frank Bennett built the CSL-M extension and the Juris-M fork of Zotero specifically to escape these restrictions, introducing a jurisdiction test attribute, five new parallel-citation attributes, a gazette-flag boolean, and a Legal Resource Registry of machine-readable jurisdiction and court identifiers. Upstream Zotero has stated it will not incorporate this functionality because it would “negatively affect the user experience for most users and dramatically impede ongoing development, all for functionality that most Zotero users don't require.”
  • Round-versus-square brackets need a per-reporter lookup. No Zotero field exists to feed this information to the processor. The rule requires knowing whether a given report series is organised by volume or by year — a classification that changes over time for some series — and CSL provides no mechanism to encode it.

Each of these limits reframes an AGLC5 drafting decision from a house-style preference to a question of whether the rule will be implementable in the tools that the AGLC's readers actually use.

The Six AGLC5 Themes and Implementability

The AGLC5 Committee has announced six consultation themes. Each intersects with implementability in ways that early engagement with tool developers could materially improve:

  1. Making the AGLC more comprehensive. The entire international materials chapter (Rules 8–14) has never been implemented in any citation tool. Looseleafs, Hansard, gazettes, and explanatory memoranda all require manual workarounds. Comprehensiveness in the printed guide has not translated to comprehensiveness in practice because the rules were not tested against the tools' data models.
  2. Improving the structure of the AGLC. Structural clarity in the guide directly determines whether rules can be encoded as deterministic logic. Rule 3.1.7, which overloads the short-title concept for both whole Acts and portions of Acts, is a structural ambiguity that no metadata schema can resolve. Restructuring the guide with tool implementers in the room would surface these conflicts before publication.
  3. Simplifying the AGLC and improving its usability. The subsequent-reference system — with its facultative (n X) convention, ambiguous short-title criteria, and “above n” / “below n” cross-references — is the single greatest source of implementation failures. Simplification here would benefit both human users and software simultaneously.
  4. Citing First Nations materials and making space for the decolonisation of legal scholarship. Any rule involving a controlled vocabulary — language names, Country names, community self-identifications — has implications for how tools represent and preserve metadata across languages and scripts. This is exactly the kind of early design choice that can go wrong quietly if tool implementers are not consulted. Getting the data model right from the start is essential to ensuring these citations are handled with the care and accuracy they deserve.
  5. Updating rules on internet materials and moving the AGLC into the digital age. This theme is the natural entry point for a generative AI citation rule, for social media posts (where CSL-level issues exist with timestamps for tweets, forum posts, and videos), and for preservation-oriented metadata. The current GenAI workaround — citing AI outputs as written correspondence under Rule 7.12 — has been propagated as interim guidance through every major university law library, demonstrating that rules-before-tools creates automation debt measured in years.
  6. Using gender-inclusive and culturally-sensitive language and examples. Citation tools render the examples that the guide provides. If sample citations in the published guide are the test corpus that tool authors build against, the examples themselves carry normative weight in software. Ensuring that the published examples reflect the Committee's values from the outset avoids the need for post-publication correction.

The AGLC4 Erratum and Community Repair

Within nine months of AGLC4's publication, a 17-page erratum was issued acknowledging errors across the 337-page guide. This is not unusual for a work of that complexity, but it does suggest that software-first review during drafting — where rules are tested against real citation data before the guide goes to print — would catch many such issues earlier and at lower cost.

Five open-source repositories currently shoulder the gap between AGLC4 and usable citation software:

  • citation-style-language/styles — the official AGLC4 CSL file, last substantive update 2020, declared by its creator to cover only Australian cases and legislation.
  • LawData-user/zotero-aglc4 — a Claude-assisted patch for dual MNC and authorised report citations, created by a practitioner who is “not a programmer.”
  • ybanens/AGLC4-CSL-with-Case-fix — a second AI-assisted patch for the same case-citation problem, created in February 2026.
  • McJones/AGLCLaTeX — a LaTeX implementation whose README catalogues its limitations with candour, listing entire jurisdictions and material types as “currently unsupported.”
  • cormacrelf/aglc4 — a fork aimed at building the style with a test corpus, noting that CSL error messages are “extremely unhelpful” and that invalid CSL “fails without a message or even a stack trace.”

Alongside these, Obiter represents a sixth major community effort: a complete AGLC4 implementation as a Word add-in, built rule by rule across all 26 chapters. The authority of this letter derives from that body of work. It is not a student asking for clarification; it is a maintainer of a functioning AGLC4 implementation asking for the input data a maintainer needs.

Concrete Requests

We respectfully ask the AGLC5 Committee to consider the following measures, each designed to ensure that the fifth edition is supported by working citation tools from the date of publication:

  1. Share draft rules with tool implementers under NDA during the drafting phase. This is low-cost for the Committee and would allow implementation issues to be identified while they can still be addressed through minor clarifications or adjustments to drafting.
  2. Accept implementation-feedback memoranda at drafting milestones. Translating citation rules into deterministic software logic is a rigorous test of their clarity and completeness. Structured feedback from implementers — identifying specific rules that prove difficult to automate and, where possible, suggesting alternative formulations that preserve the rule's intent — would strengthen the finished guide.
  3. Publish a machine-readable companion to the printed guide. A JSON, YAML, or Markdown expression of the rules — sufficient for a CSL author or Word add-in author to work from alongside the printed guide — would dramatically reduce the time between publication and working tool support. Even publishing the full sample citation library as an official Committee artefact would close much of the implementation gap.
  4. Release the AGLC5 Summary of Changes before publication, not after. The AGLC4 Summary of Changes, organised rule by rule, was published on release. A similar document circulated to implementers during the drafting stage would allow tool authors to begin preparing their implementations before the guide reaches users.
  5. Invite the open-source citation community to an implementer review. The Obiter project, the CSL maintainers, and the Juris-M team collectively represent the people who will translate AGLC5 into the software that researchers, students, and practitioners actually use. A single consultation session — even informal — would yield substantial returns in implementation quality.

An Opportunity, Not a Criticism

The AGLC has been an indispensable resource for Australian legal citation for over 25 years. The community repair work documented above is itself evidence of the standard's importance: people invest effort in fixing AGLC4 tooling precisely because the AGLC matters. This letter is a proposal to make the next edition even stronger by involving the people who build the tools during the drafting process, not after it.

The cost of consulting implementers during drafting is near zero. The cost of not doing so is measurable in years of patches, erratum pages, and AI-generated workarounds. The AGLC5 Committee has a closing window in which the fifth edition can be the first one designed with its software implementation in mind. We hope the Committee will consider opening that window to us.

Respectfully,

Matthew Watt
Author of Obiter
