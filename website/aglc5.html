Supporting AGLC5 Adoption

An open letter requesting collaboration between the AGLC5 Committee and the Obiter project to ensure citation tooling is ready from day one.

To:

  • The Melbourne University Law Review Association
  • The Melbourne Journal of International Law
  • The AGLC5 Committee

Citation standards and citation tools have historically been developed independently. A guide is published; tool authors then reverse-engineer the rules into software; researchers wait — sometimes years — for their tools to catch up. With each new edition the gap widens, because the rules grow more nuanced while the tools' data models stay the same. The demand for better tooling is real: two independent AGLC4 patches, both generated with the assistance of generative AI, are currently hosted on GitHub and being used by Australian legal researchers — not because existing tools are poorly made, but because the distance between what the AGLC requires and what any tool can produce without guidance from the standard's authors is too great for the community to bridge alone. The forthcoming fifth edition has an opportunity to close that gap by involving tool implementers during drafting.

Obiter is a free, open-source Microsoft Word add-in that implements the full AGLC4 specification across all 26 chapters. Building it rule by rule has given me a detailed understanding of where citation logic is clear enough to automate confidently and where ambiguity forces tool authors to guess. That experience is what motivates this letter: I believe it would be useful to the Committee, and I would like to offer it.

The Opportunity

Since its first edition in 1998, the Australian Guide to Legal Citation has provided the consistency and rigour that Australian legal scholarship depends on. Earlier editions did not fail — they simply predated the tooling ecosystem that exists today. AGLC5 can be the first edition designed with implementability as a drafting consideration, because the tools and their user base have matured to a point where the collaboration is now practical and worthwhile.

AGLC5 would not be the first citation standard developed with tool implementers in the room. OSCOLA 5 was drafted alongside consultation with the teams behind Zotero and other reference managers, and New Zealand’s NZLSG 3 was similarly developed with implementability in mind. The result in both cases was faster, more accurate tool support from day one.

Three areas illustrate what that collaboration could look like for AGLC5:

  • Generative AI citation. AGLC4 predates ChatGPT and contains no rule for generative AI outputs. The Melbourne University Law Review editors issued interim guidance advising researchers to cite AI outputs broadly following Rule 7.12 (written correspondence), and every major Australian university law library has since propagated that workaround through their LibGuides. No citation tool can emit a correct GenAI citation today because no rule authorises one. If AGLC5 introduces a GenAI rule — as the Committee's “digital age” theme suggests it may — early consultation with tool authors would ensure that working software support is available from day one rather than years later.
  • Subsequent references. The (n X) cross-reference system introduced in AGLC4 is one of the most common citation patterns researchers encounter, and it has proven to be among the most difficult to automate reliably. The AGLC4 preface acknowledges the system is “facultative rather than mandatory,” but the rules do not specify a computable criterion for when short titles should appear or how tools should distinguish short titles for whole Acts from short titles for portions of Acts (Rule 3.1.7). If AGLC5 revisits subsequent references — as the “simplification” theme invites — tool implementers could help identify formulations that are both editorially sound and reliably automatable.
  • International materials. The international materials chapters (Rules 8–14) — treaties, UN documents, ICJ decisions, international arbitration, and supranational materials — have never been fully implemented in any citation tool. The data models that tools use to store citation metadata do not have fields that map naturally to these material types. If the Committee's goal of making AGLC5 more comprehensive includes strengthening this chapter, early dialogue with tool authors about metadata representation would help ensure that comprehensiveness in the guide translates to comprehensiveness in practice.

The Six AGLC5 Themes

The AGLC5 Committee has announced six consultation themes. Each intersects with implementability in ways where tool authors could contribute constructively:

  1. Making the AGLC more comprehensive. New material types are only truly comprehensive when the tools researchers use can represent and format them. Early review of proposed rules against existing metadata models would identify gaps before publication.
  2. Improving the structure of the AGLC. Structural clarity in the guide directly determines whether rules can be encoded as deterministic logic. Restructuring with tool implementers in the room would surface ambiguities — such as fields that must carry two distinct meanings — before they become implementation problems.
  3. Simplifying the AGLC and improving its usability. Rules that are simpler for human users are almost always simpler to automate. Simplification of the subsequent-reference system, for example, would benefit researchers and software simultaneously.
  4. Citing First Nations materials and making space for the decolonisation of legal scholarship. Any rule involving a controlled vocabulary — language names, Country names, community self-identifications — has implications for how tools represent and preserve metadata across languages and scripts. Getting the data model right from the start is essential to ensuring these citations are handled with the care and accuracy they deserve.
  5. Updating rules on internet materials and moving the AGLC into the digital age. This theme is the natural entry point for a generative AI citation rule, for social media formats, and for preservation-oriented metadata such as content hashes and archived URLs. Tool authors can help ensure new digital rules are automatable from day one.
  6. Using gender-inclusive and culturally-sensitive language and examples. Citation tools render the examples the guide provides. If sample citations are the test corpus that tool authors build against, the examples carry normative weight in software. Ensuring the published examples reflect the Committee's values from the outset avoids the need for post-publication correction.

The AGLC4 experience offers a useful reference point: within nine months of publication, a 17-page erratum was issued acknowledging corrections across the guide. This is not unusual for a work of that complexity, but it does suggest that testing rules against real citation data during drafting — something tool implementers do as a matter of course — could catch many such issues earlier and at lower cost.

What I Am Asking

I propose five measures, each designed to ensure that the fifth edition is supported by working citation tools from the date of publication:

  1. Share draft rules with tool implementers under NDA during the drafting phase. This is low-cost for the Committee and would allow implementation issues to be identified while they can still be addressed through minor clarifications or adjustments to drafting.
  2. Accept implementation-feedback memoranda at drafting milestones. Translating citation rules into deterministic software logic is a rigorous test of their clarity and completeness. Structured feedback from implementers — identifying specific rules that prove difficult to automate and, where possible, suggesting alternative formulations that preserve the rule's intent — would strengthen the finished guide.
  3. Publish a machine-readable companion to the printed guide. A structured expression of the rules — sufficient for a citation-style author or add-in developer to work from alongside the printed guide — would dramatically reduce the time between publication and working tool support. Even publishing the sample citation library as an official Committee artefact would close much of the gap.
  4. Release the AGLC5 Summary of Changes before publication, not after. The AGLC4 Summary of Changes was published on release. A similar document circulated to implementers during drafting would allow tool authors to begin preparing their implementations before the guide reaches users.
  5. Invite the open-source citation community to an implementer review. A single consultation session — even informal — would yield substantial returns in implementation quality and ensure that the people who translate AGLC5 into working software have the context they need to do so accurately.

What I Am Offering

In return, I can offer the Committee a concrete testing service: Obiter’s AGLC4 engine covers all 26 chapters, backed by over 500 unit tests drawn from the guide’s own examples. Any draft AGLC5 rule shared under NDA can be encoded, tested against real citation data, and returned with a structured report identifying ambiguities, edge cases, and formatting conflicts — before the rule reaches publication. That feedback loop is what turns a citation guide into a citation guide that tools can implement on day one.

Beyond testing, I can offer structured feedback on draft rules as they are developed, and a public commitment to making AGLC5 support free and available to every Australian researcher, student, and practitioner. I am not asking for a seat on the Committee. I am asking for the input data that tool authors need to do their best work — and offering my work in return.

The AGLC has been an indispensable resource for Australian legal citation for over 25 years. The investment that the open-source community has made in building AGLC4 tooling is itself evidence of the standard’s importance. AGLC5 can be the strongest edition yet, and a small amount of collaboration during drafting will make a meaningful difference to how quickly and accurately the new rules reach the researchers who depend on them.

Respectfully,

Matthew Watt
Author of Obiter
